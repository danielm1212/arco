## Warstwa 1 — inżynieria samego powiadomienia

Because the first layer is the engineering behind the notification itself. Most people think the secret is personalization. That's what everyone says: you have to make things custom to your users — "Enrico, view some memories". Well, this doesn't work. You probably learned this from the thousands of emails with your name you get every single day. See, the secret to making notifications that change your behavior is not personalization. **It's specificity.**

So, instead of generic memories, let's add a little photo preview and make it about something relevant that happened today some years ago. Four years ago, I took this trip with my friends — so let's choose that. So, how do you detect which year's photos are actually a meaningful memory? Well, first we check how many pictures the user took that day. Usually cool trips, parties, overall great days are the ones with the most photos on average. So let's start by ordering the years based on that. Now, Google of course knows where you live because, well, Google. So from EXIF data, which is also stored in Google Photos, we can know when pictures are taken in a place where you don't live — so it's a trip. But these may all be boring photos of documents you took that day. Since Google Photos does visual analysis (for example, to help with search), it builds a set of tags for each photo. So we can take a common theme like skiing or mountain environment and create a memory based on that. In our notification, we add this in as well. Now let's run it through a basic large language model to make the tag a bit more playful. And our notification now looks much better. This is real personalization — it's specificity. The more specific the detail, the harder it is to look past; but also the more complex it is to build. And once you start to notice this, you cannot unnotice it. This simple-looking notification requires deep historical analysis of your photos, visual recognition, personal data about yourself, a layer of AI processing — all of this working for millions of people every single day.

But that's not enough. Take a look at this. By the rule of specificity we should be more specific — tell me who viewed my video. But they do this on purpose, because the second principle is something our psychology feeds on: **the curiosity gap.** In the case of Loom, the question they put in my mind is: did my boss or some other co-worker see my video? I need to know who that is. It's about building questions in your mind, and every product does it differently.

Now, take a look at this. This is an actual notification I got from Hinge. Notice we have the name Nadia. In this case the curiosity gap question would be "who liked me?" — but if it looks like this, the question changes to "is Nadia hot?" And yes, Nadia was hot. But I started telling her about notifications and for some reason she blocked me.

But making you curious still isn't the same as making you take action. Notice it doesn't say "come learn Spanish". It says "don't lose your streak". This is the psychological principle of **loss aversion**: we're more afraid to lose something we already have than to gain something new. It's what's behind these — none of them are offering you something; they're threatening to take something away. Like the daily opportunity to post something.

Now you might think another factor is timing — when these are actually sent. And you'd be right, but it's not truly a matter of timing; **it's a matter of context.** Duolingo doesn't remind you at a fixed time. It waits 23.5 hours after your last session, because that's the exact moment yesterday you did your Duolingo stuff — you already proved that at that time you were free. But it also caps it at 10 p.m., so it's not sent too late when you're already asleep. They proved that just getting the timing right, nothing else, can lift taps by around 60%. And even for messaging apps, which look straightforward (you get a message → send a notification), it's much more complex than it seems. For example, this is the flowchart Slack uses to decide whether to send a notification at all.

Because while a notification might seem meaningless, it's the single most powerful tool products have to build habits. BeReal only exists because of this notification. Duolingo probably as well. While basically everything else you do with your tech requires you to do something — open an app, go to a website, take an action — **notifications are the only push lever products have.** Build them wrong and users will just uninstall your product. But learn the principles behind them and you'll be able to shape user behavior at scale.

## Warstwa 2 — dlaczego to działa (historia i psychologia)

Layer two is where we find out why notifications have become so powerful. And it all has to do with this: the BlackBerry's blinking dot. Some of you remember the glorious era where phones had a dedicated little light just for notifications. This one light was so good at making people check their phones that it coined a term: the CrackBerry. This was a time when a notification meant one very specific thing: you need to do something right now, in the next minute. This is how notifications looked in iOS 3, the first version where they existed — a full-screen annoying popup taking over the entire screen with no notification center. It only meant: you have to do something now. And that made sense, because notifications were meant for something like an incoming message.

In a study, researchers went through over 200 million real notifications from users. Even today, a large part of them is still like this — the one-minute notification for something you have to do right now. These are your SMS, WhatsApp; on average they get tapped around 15 seconds from when you see them. On the other end of the spectrum you have email. Technically a notification and an email are kind of the same — something that arrives instantaneously. But email is different because of the inbox. You already know that even if you don't read something right now, it will sit there forever and pile up. Email clients have been giving us spam filters, tagging, labels and folders, trying to fix this growing pile. Email is designed to be dealt with later.

So for years there were only really two kinds of pings: act now, or deal with it later. But that same study on 200 million notifications found something weird: only 26% of notifications that appeared at the top made people stop and tap. Instead, there's another zone — for stuff you're not going to do right now, not the next day, but maybe in the next hour. And this area is probably one of the most powerful ways your tech manipulates you into doing what they want within the next hour. Because it's not really about a single notification — **it's about repetition.** The first time Duolingo tells you to do their lesson, that's a simple notification. But once you've done it seven days in a row, their data shows you're already 2.4 times more likely to come back tomorrow on your own. Keep it going, and at some point you're not opening Duolingo because it pinged you — you're opening it because not doing it feels wrong. There's a name for this; it's the entire premise of the book "Hooked" on building habit-forming products.

So if notifications create this addictive behavior, what if you just stopped them? Researchers had people switch off every single notification on their phone. Yes, they got more done — but they also felt more anxious, weirdly lonely, felt like they were missing out and letting other people down. Every single person in the study turned them back on. And switching them off didn't even stop people from checking their phones. As addictive as scrolling is, it still needs you to get bored, pick up your phone and open the app yourself — that's pull. The app is just sitting there waiting for you. **A notification is the only thing that's push.** It doesn't wait for you to get bored; it reaches into your pocket and creates the moment on the product's schedule, not yours.

And just like every technology, this is not inherently good or evil. You can use it to build habit-forming products around something good — like learning a language, building a new workout habit, feeling more competitive in sports. But at the same time, this habit formation can be used to monetize you. One of my favorite notifications, one I open almost every time, is the Google Photos one. It lets me relive moments with my friends and memories of things we did together years ago. I genuinely love it — even though, yes, Google Photos is built so you end up subscribing to one of their storage plans and locking into the ecosystem.

## Warstwa 3 — AI pomiędzy produktem a Tobą

Ever since the BlackBerry days, notifications have been the direct push way our tech communicated with us. But now, for the first time ever, things are changing.

This is how things have been so far: the product fires a notification, it lands on your device, you read it. But look — all the time we spent in layer 1 creating the perfect notification has been nuked by Apple's summarization feature, which summarized it back into a boring, unclicky notification — exactly the type that doesn't work. We're starting to see something new. Apple and Google both shipped their version of this, depending on the OS and, on Android, the manufacturer. Every notification you get now runs through a model on your phone first; before it reaches you it can reword it, reorder it, shorten it. It's not the case now, but it may even decide you don't need to see a notification at all. Google even holds a patent on a system that picks whether and when to deliver a notification in the first place.

And this has already caused real damage. When Apple's AI summarized a BBC news article, it told millions of people that a man had shot himself — and the BBC never reported that. AI just invented it and put it next to the BBC app's logo on millions of home screens. Apple had to scramble and switch the feature off for news. That's how much power this little thing already has. On email, this is already here. And on an even funnier level: sales and marketing departments — even your co-workers — use AI to write emails and docs, and then you use AI to summarize and filter them back.

There are entire products that do nothing but sit between you and your messages, rewriting them, filtering them, summarizing them. Websites are getting stuffed with descriptions, pages and whole files (like llms.txt) meant to be read not by humans but by AI agents. If your notifications are managed by Google's or Apple's system, it might make sense to introduce metadata invisible to humans — not the text of the notification, but guidance for your agent-secretary on whether to show it, in which context, and whether to change the wording. We are going in this direction, and eventually everything we did in layer 1 will become a relic of the past — because it won't be about humans building habits anymore. It'll be about convincing their agents to show them something that builds a habit. We'll go from building products for people to building products for people's AI agents.

But behind every product you use every day, there's something else that's hidden — a set of decisions that are not design, not engineering, but probably the least talked about and most important part of the tech you use every day.
