After studying nearly 3,000 paywalls, hundreds of subscription flows on Mobbin, and talking to someone who's designed over 4,700 paywalls, we found this: sometimes an ugly paywall could outperform a beautiful one. "No matter what I did, the ugly paywall with a ton of text completely overperformed anything that I would do." More friction could increase conversions. Multi-page paywalls almost always do better than single-page paywalls. Every paywall today kind of looks the same — so I wanted to know what separates a high-performing paywall from an average one.

## Decyzja zapada przed paywallem

I used to think the decision happens on a paywall, but actually users sometimes decide to pay before they even see the paywall. All the different touchpoints are extremely important. The paywall can appear after onboarding, when you unlock a premium feature, inside settings, or even as a win-back offer before you leave. Those paywalls speak more to the user because they're more personalized. You're constantly trying to get the user to either pay or stay as a subscribing member, so you have to think about the entire lifecycle of the customer. Yes, product is important for solving a problem, but lifecycle monetization and the paywalls — that's the only part of the app that makes money.

**A paywall isn't just a screen. It's a flow.** Before asking for money, Opal spends time selling the outcome — getting 8 years of your life back. By the time you reach the paywall, it doesn't feel like an interruption. By showing users how many years of their life they could save, trial sign-ups went up from 7% to 17%.

Jonathan prefers multi-page paywalls: "Multi-page paywalls almost always do better than single-page paywalls. In this paywall you're at the end of the customer journey and onboarding, and you've just showed the customer: hey, your plan is ready, this is what you can expect. You're speaking to them from an emotional perspective — you're telling them they're going to start feeling like themselves in 4 weeks, something in line with their actual ideal outcome. Does the paywall already feel part of the experience? If it's an onboarding, is it a natural segue to finish the onboarding?"

Ahead takes a similar approach: information unfolds gradually, giving users time to process the decision instead of forcing them to absorb everything at once. When a paywall works, it doesn't feel like you've hit a wall — it feels like the product is asking you to take the natural next step.

## Timing i redukcja ryzyka

Even a well-designed paywall shown at the wrong moment is still a bad paywall. One example is the one-time offer: if you close it, it's gone. Paired with animation and haptic feedback, it pulls attention exactly when you're considering an upgrade.

Even when timing is right, people hesitate: will I forget to cancel? Am I making the right decision? That's why some of the best-performing paywalls focus on **reducing risk**. Blinkist had a problem: users complained about free trials because they felt tricked into being charged. So they redesigned the paywall to show a **step-by-step timeline** (of the trial). The result: more trial sign-ups, fewer complaints. Push-notification opt-ins also went up — which makes sense, because they remind you before your trial ends.

Tipstop emphasized the free trial, added a discount badge, and made the offer easier to understand. The offer didn't change — the way it was presented did. Direct conversions almost tripled.

And then there's Slopes. Instead of redesigning the paywall, they asked: what if we got rid of the paywall altogether? Inspired by Apple's "redeem free trial" approach, they built what they call a **pay ramp**: users see a single action — redeem your free week — no traditional paywall standing in the way. This alone increased trial starts by 25%.

One of Jonathan's favorite tweaks: "This **'no commitment, cancel anytime'** subtitle always seems to do well — adding it bumps things up incrementally. Having a call to action describing what the user is doing instead of just 'Continue' is hit or miss, but I like to experiment with it. Also: the right chevron on the button — I can't judge yet whether it's causal, but most of the paywalls that end up winning have it, so I include it along with that subtitle."

Exit intent: "If they hit cancel, I like to show an exit-intent sheet — if they're not ready to commit for a year, they can still subscribe to the monthly plan." On aggressive last-minute discounts: "I wouldn't do this outside maybe Cyber Monday/Black Friday. To stay compliant and safe, I'd recommend instead an offer with a **larger trial**." Giving users a longer trial achieves the same goal while preserving trust.

## Friction bywa dobre

If reducing risk helps conversions, you'd expect removing friction to do the same. But that's not the case. To start a free trial on Outsider you need to enter credit card details: sign-ups dropped by more than half, but conversion increased five times — more than double the paying customers. The extra friction filtered out people who were never serious. Moonly discovered something similar: they introduced a free trial, but **only on the annual plan**.

## Framing oferty

Sometimes what changes isn't the offer — it's how it's framed. The most common technique: **social proof** with real user reviews and a five-star rating ("these social accolades show credibility and authority"). Second: **value framing** — framing value so it matches what users care about; all these screens are selling different futures. Then **price anchoring**: Tide breaks the price into smaller weekly amounts; Ahead compares its subscription to things people already pay for, like coffee or therapy.

## Pricing i pakiety

Headspace tested 7-, 14- and 30-day free trials. The winner: **14-day trial on the annual plan** — even though annual costs more, the longer trial made the decision feel less risky.

Best pricing structure? It depends what you optimize for: "Ideally you default to a yearly product — it has the highest LTV. If you need more options, pair yearly with weekly or monthly, but **only show two** to decrease cognitive load. If you want more pricing options, hide them behind a 'view all plans' button that opens a sheet. Keep the base paywall simple; get users in to see how base stats perform before optimizing all your placements."

Jonathan is a big believer in **table-based paywalls**: "I'm a huge fan of tables. They do a good job of showcasing what you're missing out on if you don't subscribe." A **video paywall** also works well — showing the app in action, a simple bullet list, simple social proof of people already using your app; video communicates value faster than static screenshots.

## Polish

"Animations work really well. There's an app going viral right now called Focus Town — they've done an amazing job of branding, an Animal Crossing-esque experience. That's the level of polish we'll be seeing from the most successful apps. The bar has been raised: now that anyone can ship an app, the ones that put in time to polish with animations, custom character design, world-building will be the most successful." When two products compete for the same user, polish could be the reason someone chooses yours.

## Anty-wzorce (działają, ale…)

Not every pattern is worth copying. Some of the highest-converting paywalls are the ones Jonathan is most skeptical of — the same tactics you find in dropshipping stores. "One paywall that works extremely well, and that I have a hard time accepting, is the **spin-the-wheel paywall** — a predetermined Lottie animation landing on some discount; the user 'won' something, so they're more likely to subscribe at that price. If you're optimizing for a long-term business, that may not be a good paywall for you. But if you're optimizing weekly revenue — unfortunately, it works. The issue: everyone is starting to use it." When every app uses fake urgency, users eventually stop believing it: "more and more users are comfortable closing paywalls because they know they'll get some offer afterwards." And in early 2026, Apple started rejecting paywall patterns relying on free-trial toggles considered confusing or misleading. Remember this is a long-term game.

Jonathan once worked with an AI girlfriend app: "They had probably the ugliest paywall I've ever seen — compact elements, a very aggressive squeeze-page paywall. It overperformed pretty much any paywall I tried." Which means **there is no universal best paywall — only better experiments.** "One thing I learned from Jake and Nick at Superwall: **design tests move the needle the most**. We ended up running radically different designs until something blows everything out of the water — a video paywall, a bullet-list paywall, a trial-timeline paywall, a long-form paywall." Test copy, pricing, flow until something clearly wins.

## Retencja > konwersja

Getting people to subscribe is one thing; building something people continue to pay for is much harder. "The metrics I wish more founders cared about most: **retention and LTV**. If retention and LTV are good, you're building a good product — and you want to build good products so the ecosystem flourishes." Maybe the question isn't "how do I get people to pay?" — it's "how could I create something worth paying for?"
